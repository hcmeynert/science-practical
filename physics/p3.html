Physics P.3: Measuring the angular Distance between the Sun and a halved Moon



Prerequisites: At night, I put a bottle into the light coming from a halved Moon and measured the length of its shadow. I also record the exact time and date, the distance of the Moon from the southern direction and the latitude of my location.

The law of cosines in spherical trigonometry reads, according to the above diagram $$ \cos x = \cos (s)\cdot \cos (n) + \sin (s)\cdot \sin (n)\cdot\cos (\xi ) $$ gives \(x\). The law of sines, in the very same spherical geometry, now reads $$ \frac{\sin x}{\sin \xi } = \frac{\sin n}{\sin \nu }$$ to furnish \(\nu\). Now, we can calculate from the table, $$ (\delta +\nu )- \nu = \delta $$ We plainly calculate the approximate height \(\varrho _S\) of the Sun above the celestial equator by $$ \sin \varrho _S = \sin (\vartheta )\cdot\sin (\varepsilon ) $$ where \(\varepsilon = 23,5^o\) is the ecliptic angle. We get $$ z = \frac{\pi }{2}- \varrho _S $$ We now are in a position to derive the angle finally sought for, that is, \(d\), by applying the law of cosines in spherical tringonometry again, namely $$ \cos d = \cos (z)\cdot \cos (x) + \sin (z)\cdot \sin (x)\cdot\cos (\delta ) $$ The following table collects all values obtained in course of calculation:

definition symbol calculated value
distance of the Moon from the north pole at that time \(x\) \(60.77810^o\)
angle of Moon-North-Zenith at north pole \(\nu \) \(53.15338^o\)
angle of Sun-North-Moon at north pole \(\delta \) \(88.84661^o\)
angle between Sun and north pole \(z\) \(95.92369^o\)
distance of the Sun and a halved Moon \(d \) \(91.88593^o\)
uncertainty = \(\pm 5^o\)


So, from the uncertainty of \(\pm 5^o\) (which is estimated because the halved Moon is not exactly halved, as well as because the distance of the Sun from equinox is approximated, and because the other errors add up), we can say with these values $$ d > 87^o $$ and, therefore, $$ \frac{d_S}{d_M} = \frac{1}{\cos d} > 20 $$ (\(d_S, d_M\) distances of Sun and Moon from Earth) So, our result reads:

The Sun is more than 20 times more distant from Earth than the Moon is.
