Physics P.1: Measuring Solar Second



Prerequisites: I used in this a pendulum wall clock, a marker for the shadow of the sun on a sunny balcony, and a tape measure to measure the length of the pendulum

1. The pendulum clock does 48:00:00 h ± 00:10:00 h in exactly two solar days (as verified with the marker for the shadow of the sun on my sunny balcony). This calculates to an error of 0.35 %. Therefore, we get a number of hours $$ h_c = 24.0 \pm 0.35 \% $$

2. My pendulum clock does 500 ± 10 oscillations in ten minutes according to my counting. Therefore, it certainly exactly does $$ N_h = 3000 $$ oscillations per hour. For, in the measured realm of 3000 ± 60 oscillations, the number 3000 is most prominent, and the clockmaker will certainly have chosen it. It can easily be achieved by gears. Furthermore, it can be divided by 12 (5 minutes) and by 60 (one minute).

3. The length of the pendulum of my clock is $$ l_p = (0.358 \pm 0.0037) \text{ m} $$ This follows, if I measure its three parts, add them and calculate the error using Theorem T.1.

4. The length \(l_s\) of a pendulum with a period equal to one solar second or \(\frac{1}{86400}\) of a solar day can now be found as $$ l_s = (0.24861 \pm 0.00303) \text{ m} $$ This follows at once, if you manipulate the well-known formula for the period \(T\) of a pendulum with length \(l\) $$ T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}} $$ to get $$ l_s = l_p\cdot\left(\frac{N_h\cdot h_c}{86400}\right)^2 $$ Using Theorem T.1 and the values for \(l_p\), \(N_h\) and \(h_c\) found before gives the error. This is what was to be found.
