The "diameter" \(d\) of the cellular nuclei hereby pertains to the blueish and greenish parts to be seen in the picture, which are surrounded by even larger parts that don't belong to the nucleus itself.
If we multiply the ratio of the pixels of the nucleus and the resolution of the long side of the picure, multiplying by 480 \(\mu \)m, and collecting the errors according to Theorem T.1, we get $$ d = (5.87 \pm 0.74) \mu\text{m}$$ This exactly coincides with the literature value to be found on various places on the internet.
Now, we are in a position to do a fascinating estimation. We estimate a priori the information content of an onion. A simple 1 MB picture of an onion has \(10^7\) bit. This is the absolute minimum. If you take a 1000 year long mp4 of the growing of the plant, which is about \(10^7\) GB or \(10^{17}\) bit, it will exceed the information content of the onion. So, we have for the number \(G\) of genes $$ 10^7 < G < 10^{17} $$ So you get for the diameter \(d_G\) of a genome of 1 bit $$ 10^8 > d_G > 10^{-11} $$ (if you calculate in cubes of \(d\) and \(d_G\)). If you now set forth, that a genome consist of, rougly estimated, 10 atoms in diameter, you get the diameter \(d_a\) of an atom as $$ 10^{-9} > d_a > 10^{-12} $$
